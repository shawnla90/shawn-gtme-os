## observations

people ask me what's the best way to build your own AI agent and I think they picture the exciting part. the weekend where you wire up the API, watch it respond for the first time, screenshot the terminal and post it. that part takes maybe 20 hours.

what nobody talks about is month two. month three. the part where your agent is running and you're just... maintaining it. watching logs. fixing edge cases. tuning prompts that drifted. updating caches. that's where most people quit. not because it's hard. because it's boring.

eleven consecutive D grades means eleven days where the system ran but nothing dramatic shipped. no new features. no architecture changes. no demos. just crons firing, data syncing, content generating. the infrastructure equivalent of a restaurant that's open every day, serving the same menu, keeping the kitchen clean.

the interesting pattern is that this boring middle is where compounding actually happens. ten LinkedIn posts didn't exist two weeks ago. now there's a backlog. the crypto tracker has weeks of signal history building up. the Reddit cache has months of post data. none of it is impressive on any single day. all of it is quietly becoming something.
