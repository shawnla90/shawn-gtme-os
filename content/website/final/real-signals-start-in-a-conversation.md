# Real Signals Start in a Conversation, Not a CRM

**tl;dr:** Yesterday's LinkedIn post said org-level "intent signals" are qualification, not intent. The thread agreed from every angle. Today, what I've been building to catch the actual signal — and why the play is using your own voice once you find it. Reveal next week.

![The signals are in public — you're just not reading them. Five-scene storyboard: the problem, the mechanism, the output, the contrast, the reveal.](/blog/storyboard-cover.gif)

## What the thread confirmed

Yesterday I wrote that every tool in the GTM stack selling "intent signals" is really selling qualification. Series B raised. VP of sales changed jobs. Org is hiring twelve SDRs. Same feed, same play, same list as your competitors.

The thread came back with exactly the shape I had been circling on my own.

Operators are qualifying by metadata and calling it urgency. The hunger is for the buyer's actual voice. The 11pm vent about the stack. The "we just switched off X, now what" thread. The founder asking if anyone has built custom enrichment because the vendors are fried.

The person typing the words in public is more qualified than any funding-round alert a platform will sell you. And almost nobody on the opposing team is reading.

"Might need" versus "does need." Six stacked org signals versus one person typing a specific complaint.

That gap is where the value lives.

## The pushback is the product

The pushback that kept coming up was the fair one: manual signal hunting doesn't scale. Teams can't hold the discipline week after week.

Right. That is the whole reason we're building.

The problem with "go read Reddit every morning" as advice is it doesn't compound. You do it for a week, something shinier shows up, the discipline dies. Meanwhile the signal keeps flowing — in public, timestamped, searchable — and nobody on your team is reading.

So the question became: how do you get the signal-to-voice loop down to a morning routine a single human can actually run.

## What I've been building

Semantic search over Reddit, surfaced as a morning mailbox.

You open it. There are your conversations for the day. People in r/sales, r/gotomarketengineering, r/SaaS, r/hubspot — wherever your ICP actually talks — who have, in the last 24 hours, described the problem your customers solve. With context, urgency scoring, and a pointer back to the thread.

Not a lead list. Not an auto-reply queue. A reading list, curated by meaning instead of by keyword.

Reveal is Monday. The name isn't the point. The mechanism is.

## Why semantic, not keywords

Keyword monitoring is what most "social listening" tools actually are. You type "HubSpot alternative" and get every post containing those words. You miss the post where someone writes "we're bleeding $1,200/mo on a CRM that does nothing" because the phrase didn't match.

Semantic search finds meaning, not text. A post describing CRM-replacement urgency matches even if the writer never uses the word "alternative." A founder complaining their enrichment stack is broken matches even if they never say "Clay."

Mechanically: every post that hits the watched subs gets embedded. The query describes the shape of the problem your customers have, not the keywords they might use. Top N every morning.

That's the entire mechanism. The taste is in which subs you watch and how the queries are shaped. The rest is plumbing.

## Why Reddit first — and why it compounds

A LinkedIn post is dead in 24 hours. Your great reply on a great thread is buried by tomorrow.

A Reddit thread that lands stays up for months. Years sometimes. AI search engines crawl it. Six months from now, when someone asks ChatGPT or Perplexity or Google's AI about your category, the answer cites Reddit — and your reply on the right thread is what gets surfaced.

So the play isn't just "find the buyer." It's "show up usefully where the answer compounds." Most of what you'll find won't be a buyer right now. It'll be someone in your category asking a question. Showing up there is the signal too. The slow one. The trust one. The one that pays off six months out when AI summarizes your space and your name is in the citation.

That recursion is the part I keep coming back to. The signal isn't only "here is someone about to buy." The signal is also "here is someone shaping how the next thousand people will think about this category — be in the room."

Reddit first because the half-life is the longest and the AI crawlers are hungry. Slack and Discord communities next — private, sharper, less crowded. LinkedIn comment threads after that. The pipeline doesn't change. The surface does.

## The part I've been thinking about all week

When AI surfaces a real buying conversation, the reflex is to reach for the next AI to draft the reply.

Don't.

If you're going to win anything in 2026, it's because you can talk to a person who is actually stuck, and they can feel that you read what they wrote. AI-drafted replies are instantly recognizable. They're also instantly archived.

The opposite stack is the one that wins. AI surfaces the conversation. You reply. In your voice. Short. Specific. Useful. Like you wandered into the thread by accident.

That's why we're not building auto-reply. We're surfacing the thirty-to-forty real conversations your ICP is having this week. You pick three to five. You show up like a human in a forum, not a marketer warming up a sequence.

Anyone can automate this. The whole point is choosing not to. The reason it works is you can choose not to — and the person on the other end can feel it.

## The co-founder math

The reason a founder + co-founder can ship a signal-capture product in April 2026 that would have taken twenty engineers in April 2024 is the hard parts moved into the model.

Scraping. Embedding. Ranking. Rendering. All of it is a Claude Code session away. What's left is taste — which subs, which signal shape, which threads belong in the morning digest, how the inbox feels when you open it at 7am.

Two of us. The product is the feed. The product is also you choosing to reply like a person.
